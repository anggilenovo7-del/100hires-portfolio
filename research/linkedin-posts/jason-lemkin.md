## Post 1
**Date:** June 9, 2026
**URL:** [Original Post](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasonmlemkin_if-you-are-at-scale-you-now-have-2-full-time-share-7444844269237956608-wJxC/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADGpi08BcD4FugyYukQKbiz0k7tPk0-E-Ko)

### **Why this post is relevant:**

This post highlights one of the biggest strategic challenges facing mature B2B SaaS companies in the AI era: balancing the needs of existing customers while simultaneously building the next generation of AI-powered products.

Jason argues that companies can no longer focus solely on optimizing their current business. Leaders must also invest in AI-driven innovation, even when it creates tension with existing products, teams, and revenue streams. The post emphasizes that successful companies must manage both objectives at the same time rather than choosing one over the other.

The post is particularly relevant because it illustrates how industry transformation creates difficult trade-offs. While protecting customer retention and expansion remains critical, companies that fail to adapt to emerging technologies risk losing market leadership to AI-native competitors. This perspective helps explain why many SaaS companies are rethinking product strategy, organizational priorities, and long-term growth plans.

Key lessons:
- SaaS companies must balance current customer success with future innovation.
- AI transformation often requires disrupting existing products and workflows.
- Internal resistance to change can become a significant competitive risk.
- Market leaders cannot rely solely on existing advantages when new technologies emerge.

## Post 2
**Date:** June 12, 2026
**URL:** [Original Post](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasonmlemkin_one-reason-its-very-hard-for-b2b-companies-share-7447299097574830081-VGuZ/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADGpi08BcD4FugyYukQKbiz0k7tPk0-E-Ko)

### **Why this post is relevant:**

This post highlights a less obvious challenge facing B2B SaaS companies in the AI era: economics. Jason argues that while many software companies are racing to add AI capabilities, they often face significantly higher operating costs than the AI platform providers themselves.

The post explains how complex AI-powered workflows can be expensive to deliver at scale. While end users may access advanced AI capabilities through relatively inexpensive subscriptions to platforms such as Claude or ChatGPT, SaaS vendors often incur substantial API costs when embedding similar functionality into their own products. This creates pricing and profitability challenges that can limit the quality of AI features they are able to offer.

The post is relevant because it encourages marketers, founders, and product leaders to think beyond AI hype and consider the business realities behind AI adoption. Competitive advantage is not only determined by technology capabilities but also by economics, pricing models, and sustainable business execution.

Key lessons:
- AI adoption creates new cost and profitability challenges for SaaS companies.
- Technology advantages alone do not guarantee a sustainable business model.
- Product strategy must consider both customer value and economic viability.
- Understanding market economics can reveal hidden competitive risks.

## Post 3
**Date:** June 13, 2026
**URL:** [Original Post](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasonmlemkin_one-of-the-toughest-things-for-many-teams-share-7447815631153942528-Gc8v/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADGpi08BcD4FugyYukQKbiz0k7tPk0-E-Ko)

### **Why this post is relevant:**

This post emphasizes the importance of recognizing and responding to technological disruption. Jason argues that many companies are falling behind in the AI era, but the greatest risk is not the disruption itself—it is refusing to acknowledge it. By being honest about competitive gaps, companies can begin making the changes necessary to remain relevant.

The post highlights a recurring theme in SaaS leadership: successful transformation starts with confronting reality. Rather than protecting existing metrics or relying on past success, leaders must evaluate whether their products continue to meet evolving customer expectations in an AI-driven market.

Jason also points out that established companies still possess a significant advantage: existing customer relationships. While AI-native competitors may move faster, incumbent companies can leverage trust, distribution, and customer knowledge if they are willing to adapt their products to changing market demands.

Key lessons:
- Acknowledging competitive threats is the first step toward meaningful change.
- Companies cannot rely solely on past success in rapidly evolving markets.
- Existing customer relationships remain a powerful strategic asset.
- AI transformation requires both urgency and willingness to rethink existing products.
