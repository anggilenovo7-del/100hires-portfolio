## Why I'm Scared to Buy SaaS Today
**URL:** [Youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bIQpzhiJ1Cw)
**Published:** March 5, 2025

### Transcript / Key Points
1. SaaS Has Forgotten the "Service" in Software-as-a-Service
Jason argues that many SaaS companies have become overly focused on retention metrics, expansion revenue, and financial optimization while neglecting customer experience. Buying software increasingly feels risky because vendors make it difficult to leave.

2. Customers Are Afraid of Vendor Lock-In
One of the biggest concerns buyers have today is not whether a product works, but whether they will be trapped after purchasing.
Examples include:
- Hidden cancellation processes
- Forced interactions with support teams
- Unexpected exit fees
- Difficult downgrade paths
- Auto-renewal traps
The harder it is to leave, the more stressful it becomes to buy.

3. AI Products Are Setting a New Standard
Products like Claude, ChatGPT, and other modern AI tools have created new customer expectations:
- Easy to try
- Easy to upgrade
- Easy to downgrade
- Easy to cancel
Jason argues that traditional SaaS vendors increasingly look outdated compared to AI-native companies that minimize friction.

4. Forced Upsells Damage Trust
Many SaaS companies introduce aggressive pricing tactics after customers become dependent on the product.
Examples include:
- Mandatory plan upgrades
- Feature gating
- Unexpected price increases
- Charging extra for previously included functionality
While these tactics may increase short-term revenue, they reduce long-term customer trust.

5. Renewal Processes Have Become Too Complicated
Jason notes that many companies now require:
- 60–90 day cancellation notices
- Multi-year commitments
- Legal threats for contract enforcement
- Automatic renewals
As a result, some organizations immediately send termination notices after signing contracts simply to avoid future renewal disputes.

6. Variable Pricing Creates Anxiety
Usage-based pricing can be powerful, but customers often struggle when they cannot predict future costs.
Examples include:
- Database-based pricing
- Contact-based pricing
- API consumption pricing
- Storage pricing
For many buyers, uncertainty is more stressful than paying a higher fixed fee.

7. Free Trials Matter More Than Ever
AI tools have reset market expectations.
Customers increasingly expect:
- Free plans
- Freemium access
- Product-led onboarding
- Immediate value before payment
Jason believes vendors that require payment before product experience create unnecessary friction.

8. Customer Success Has Become an Upsell Function
A recurring frustration is that many Customer Success teams no longer focus on helping customers succeed.
Instead, they often focus on:
- Expansion revenue
- Upselling
- Cross-selling
Customers increasingly feel that QBRs and account reviews are sales meetings disguised as support.

9. Migration Costs Are Often Ignored
Many SaaS vendors underestimate how difficult it is for customers to switch.
Customers worry about:
- Data migration
- Integrations
- Training
- Workflow changes
- Operational disruptions
Even a superior product may lose deals if migration feels too risky.

10. Time Is the Most Valuable Resource
Jason repeatedly emphasizes that buyers care less about software pricing and more about the time cost associated with:
- Demos
- Sales calls
- Procurement processes
- Negotiations
- Onboarding
The best products minimize customer effort rather than maximizing sales interactions.

### **Why this video is relevant:**
This video provides a valuable counterpoint to many modern SaaS growth strategies that prioritize retention metrics, expansion revenue, and aggressive monetization.

Jason Lemkin highlights an often-overlooked reality:
"Every piece of friction added to retention also adds friction to acquisition."

As AI-native products raise expectations around simplicity and self-service experiences, SaaS companies that make buying, using, and leaving their products easy may gain a significant competitive advantage.

For founders, product leaders, and marketers, the video reinforces several important principles:
- Customer trust compounds over time.
- Reducing friction can be a growth strategy.
- Retention tactics should not come at the expense of customer experience.
- Product-led growth works best when customers can experience value quickly.
- The easiest product to leave is often the easiest product to buy.
